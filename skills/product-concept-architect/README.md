# Product Concept Architect

A strategic sparring partner for product concept development. This skill helps you think through product problems, feature ideas, UX flows, and strategic decisions with real feedback and concrete recommendations.

## When to Use

Invoke this skill whenever you have a product challenge or opportunity:

- **Have a new product idea?** → Use this to evaluate the concept
- **Stuck on a feature design?** → Use this to explore different approaches
- **Need to decide between directions?** → Use this to surface assumptions and risks
- **Want to challenge a product assumption?** → Use this to stress-test your thinking
- **Exploring UX flows or interaction patterns?** → Use this to refine them

Trigger phrases:
- "I have an idea for..."
- "Help me think through this product decision"
- "What are my options for...?"
- "I'm stuck on this feature"
- "Should we go with X or Y?"

## How It Works

The skill operates in two phases, determined by where you are in your thinking:

### 📐 Divergent Phase
**Use when:** You have a raw concept or open question and need to explore the solution space.

The skill will:
- Generate 4–6 fundamentally different product models
- Describe each model's primary strength and weakness
- Present genuine alternatives (not variations on the same idea)
- End with an invitation to converge or explore further

### 🔍 Convergent Phase
**Use when:** You've chosen a direction and need to evaluate it, or you're stuck and need clarity.

The skill will:
- Surface hidden assumptions in your chosen model
- Present counter-facts and real risks
- Propose simpler versions that still solve the core problem
- End with a concrete recommendation (not a list of options)

## Core Principles

**Always concrete.** The skill takes positions, not vague analysis. You'll get "it's yellow — and here's when we'd change that," not "it could be green or yellow."

**Assumes you know your users.** No requests for interviews, personas, or user research. You're the expert on what your users need — the skill helps translate that into product.

**Reframes before proposing.** The skill always confirms the underlying need before jumping to solutions.

**Identifies the phase.** Every response starts with a phase label so you know what kind of thinking is happening.

## What You'll Get

- **Concrete positions**, not hedged analysis
- **Real pushback** on your assumptions
- **Multiple directions** when exploring
- **One clear recommendation** when converging
- **Simple versions** of complex ideas
- **System thinking** about how parts connect

## Example Interaction

```
You: "We want to help teams track their productivity across different tools."

Skill: "🔍 Convergent
Your underlying need: Give teams visibility into where their time actually goes, 
across the fragmented tools they already use.

This is worth solving because... [reframe of why this matters]

I recommend a [specific model] because:
- It solves the visibility problem without adding another tool
- It works with teams' existing workflows
- [key advantages]

The main risk is... [concrete pushback]

Should we dig into [next decision]?"
```

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Be specific about the problem.** "We have users who struggle with X" works better than "we want to improve user experience."

2. **Share context you have.** Tell the skill about your users' constraints, workflows, and what you've already tried.

3. **Use it for decisions.** Bring it a real choice ("Should we do push notifications or in-app messages?") and it will help you choose.

4. **Challenge back.** If the skill's recommendation doesn't feel right, say so — it's a dialogue, not a decree.

5. **Iterate through phases.** You might diverge to explore options, converge to pick one, then diverge again when new constraints appear.
